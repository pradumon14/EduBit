📖 Chemistry Notebook: Unveiling Chemical Reactions & Equations

🔬 The Dance of Atoms: What IS a Chemical Reaction?

Consider daily life: milk souring, iron rusting, food cooking, digestion, respiration. In all these, the initial substances change their nature and identity. This is a chemical change, and when it occurs, we say a chemical reaction has taken place. It's a process where reactants transform into new products with different properties, involving the breaking and forming of chemical bonds.

My Short Explanation 💬

Think of it like LEGOs! Reactants are your starting LEGO pieces. A chemical reaction is when you take them apart (break bonds) and rebuild them into something new (form new bonds) – that's your product! The new structure looks and behaves differently than the original pieces.

Activity 1.1: Burning Magnesium Ribbon

Procedure: Clean a magnesium ribbon (3-4 cm) with sandpaper. Hold it with tongs and burn it using a spirit lamp or burner, collecting the ash in a watch-glass. (Teacher assistance needed, wear eye protection.)

Observation: Magnesium ribbon burns with a dazzling white flame and changes into a white powder.

Conclusion: This white powder is magnesium oxide (MgO), formed by the reaction between magnesium and oxygen from the air.

How can we tell if a chemical reaction has occurred? Look for these tell-tale signs:

  • Change in State: E.g., Solid wax burns to form gaseous CO2 and water vapor.
  • Change in Color: E.g., Iron (silvery) rusting to reddish-brown.
  • Evolution of a Gas: E.g., Bubbles when zinc reacts with acid.
  • Change in Temperature: (Exothermic = hotter, Endothermic = colder).
  • Formation of a Precipitate: An insoluble solid formed when solutions mix.

Tips & Tricks! 💡

Not all changes are chemical! Melting ice is a physical change (state change, but still H2O). If a new substance with new properties is formed, it's likely chemical.

Activity 1.2: Lead Nitrate + Potassium Iodide

Procedure: Take lead nitrate solution in a test tube. Add potassium iodide solution.

Observation: A yellow precipitate is formed.

Activity 1.3: Zinc Granules + Acid

Procedure: Take a few zinc granules. Add dilute HCl or H2SO4. (Handle acid with care.)

Observation: Bubbles of gas (H2). Flask may feel warm.

✍️ Chemical Shorthand: Mastering Equations

A chemical equation is the chemist's concise way to describe a reaction.

Word Equations vs. Symbol Equations

Word: Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide

Symbol (skeletal): Mg + O2 → MgO

The Golden Rule: Balancing with the Law of Conservation of Mass

Atoms aren't created or destroyed, just rearranged. So, atom counts must be equal on both sides. We use coefficients (numbers in front) to balance.

My Short Explanation 💬

Balancing is like making sure you have the same number of each type of LEGO brick before and after you build something new. If you start with 2 red bricks and 4 blue bricks, your final creation must also use exactly 2 red and 4 blue bricks, just arranged differently!

Example: Balancing Fe + H2O → Fe3O4 + H2 (NCERT Method)

Step I: Draw boxes around each formula. (Do not change anything inside boxes). This is an instructional step. [Fe] + [H2O] → [Fe3O4] + [H2] Step II: List atoms of each element on LHS and RHS. Element | LHS Atoms | RHS Atoms -------------------------------- Fe | 1 | 3 H | 2 | 2 O | 1 | 4 Step III: Start with the compound having max atoms ([Fe3O4]), and element with max atoms in it (Oxygen). To balance Oxygen (1 on LHS, 4 on RHS): Put coefficient '4' before [H2O]. [Fe] + 4[H2O] → [Fe3O4] + [H2] (Partly balanced) Step IV: Balance Hydrogen (now 8 on LHS from 4[H2O], 2 on RHS). Make H2 on RHS as 4[H2]. [Fe] + 4[H2O] → [Fe3O4] + 4[H2] (Partly balanced) Step V: Balance Iron (1 on LHS, 3 on RHS). Take 3 atoms of Fe on LHS. 3[Fe] + 4[H2O] → [Fe3O4] + 4[H2] Step VI: Check correctness. (Fe:3, H:8, O:4 on both sides). Equation is balanced. Step VII: Writing Physical States (s, l, g, aq). For this reaction with steam: 3Fe(s) + 4H2O(g) → Fe3O4(s) + 4H2(g)

Tips & Tricks! 💡 Balancing Equations

  • Balance metals first, then non-metals (excluding H and O).
  • Balance hydrogen next, then oxygen last (oxygen often appears in multiple compounds).
  • If you see a polyatomic ion (like SO42-, NO3-) that appears unchanged on both sides, you can often balance it as a single unit.
  • Use the smallest whole number coefficients. If you end up with fractions, multiply the entire equation by the denominator to clear them.
  • Double-check your atom count for every element at the end!

Making Equations Even More Informative:

  • Indicating Physical States: (s), (l), (g), (aq). Example:
    2Mg(s) + O2(g) → 2MgO(s)
  • Specifying Reaction Conditions: Heat (Δ), light, catalyst etc. above/below arrow. Example:
    CaCO3(s) --Δ→ CaO(s) + CO2(g)
    Example:
    6CO2(aq) + 12H2O(l) --Sunlight, Chlorophyll→ C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) + 6H2O(l)

🗂️ Reaction Families: Classifying Chemical Changes

Chemical reactions involve breaking and making of bonds.

My Short Explanation 💬 Reaction Types Overview

Think of reaction types like different dance moves atoms can do:

  • Combination: Two dancers join to become one pair. (A + B → AB)
  • Decomposition: One pair splits into individual dancers. (AB → A + B)
  • Displacement: One stronger dancer cuts in and takes another's partner. (A + BC → AC + B)
  • Double Displacement: Two pairs swap partners. (AB + CD → AD + CB)

Two or more substances combine to form a single new substance.

Activity 1.4: Quicklime + Water

Procedure: Take a small amount of calcium oxide (quicklime) in a beaker. Slowly add water. Touch the beaker.

Observation: The beaker becomes hot. Calcium oxide reacts vigorously with water. CaO(s) + H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq) + Heat (Calcium oxide + Water → Calcium hydroxide (Slaked lime))

Conclusion: This is a combination reaction and also an exothermic reaction.

Other Examples:

  • Burning of coal: C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g)
  • Formation of water: 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l)

Exothermic Reactions: Reactions in which heat is released along with product formation. Burning of natural gas (CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)) and respiration (C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) → 6CO2(aq) + 6H2O(l) + energy) are exothermic. Decomposition of vegetable matter into compost is also exothermic.

One substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances. Often needs energy.

1. Thermal Decomposition (Heat):

Activity 1.5: Heating Ferrous Sulfate

Procedure: Take ~2g ferrous sulfate crystals (FeSO4·7H2O) in a dry boiling tube. Note initial green color. Heat over a flame. (Do not point tube at anyone, waft gas gently.)

Observation: Green color changes. Characteristic odor of burning sulfur. 2FeSO4(s) --Δ→ Fe2O3(s) + SO2(g) + SO3(g) (Ferrous sulfate (initially green) → Ferric oxide (brown) + Sulfur dioxide + Sulfur trioxide)

Note: FeSO4 crystals first lose water of crystallization.

Activity 1.6: Heating Lead Nitrate

Procedure: Take ~2g lead nitrate powder in a boiling tube. Heat over a flame.

Observation: Emission of brown fumes of nitrogen dioxide (NO2). 2Pb(NO3)2(s) --Δ→ 2PbO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g) (Lead nitrate (white) → Lead oxide (yellow) + Nitrogen dioxide (brown) + Oxygen)

Another example: CaCO3(s) --Δ→ CaO(s) + CO2(g) (Limestone to Quicklime)

2. Electrolytic Decomposition (Electricity):

Activity 1.7: Electrolysis of Water

Procedure: Take a plastic mug, drill holes, fit rubber stoppers with carbon electrodes. Fill with water (add few drops dil. H2SO4). Invert test tubes filled with water over electrodes. Pass current (6V battery).

Observation: Bubbles at both electrodes. Gas collected in one test tube is double the volume of the other. Testing with burning candle: gas with double volume burns with pop sound (Hydrogen), other gas makes candle burn brighter (Oxygen).

2H2O(l) --Electricity→ 2H2(g) + O2(g)

3. Photolytic Decomposition (Light):

Activity 1.8: Silver Chloride in Sunlight

Procedure: Take ~2g silver chloride (white) in a china dish. Place in sunlight.

Observation: White silver chloride turns grey. 2AgCl(s) --Sunlight→ 2Ag(s) + Cl2(g)

Similarly for silver bromide: 2AgBr(s) --Sunlight→ 2Ag(s) + Br2(g). These are used in black & white photography.

Endothermic Reactions: Reactions requiring energy (heat, light, electricity) are endothermic. Example: Barium hydroxide + Ammonium chloride (test tube becomes cold).

  2. Why is the amount of gas collected in one of the test tubes in Activity 1.7 double of the amount collected in the other? Name this gas. (Answer: Hydrogen gas, because water (H2O) has two parts hydrogen and one part oxygen by volume).

In a displacement reaction, a more reactive element displaces (or "kicks out") a less reactive element from its compound (usually in solution).

Tips & Tricks! 💡 Reactivity Series

To predict displacement, you need the reactivity series! A common mnemonic: "Please Stop Calling Me A Zebra, Instead Try Learning How Copper Saves Gold." (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe, Sn, Pb, H, Cu, Ag, Au). Elements higher up can displace those lower down from their salt solutions.

Activity 1.9: Iron Nails in Copper Sulfate

Procedure: Take three iron nails, clean them. Take two test tubes (A & B) with ~10mL copper sulfate solution each. Immerse two iron nails tied with a thread in test tube B for ~20 mins. Keep one nail aside.

Observation: Iron nails become brownish. Blue color of copper sulfate in test tube B fades. Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s) (Copper sulfate (blue) + Iron → Iron sulfate (light green) + Copper (brownish))

Other Examples:

  • Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
  • Pb(s) + CuCl2(aq) → PbCl2(aq) + Cu(s)

(Zinc and lead are more reactive than copper).

In a double displacement reaction, two compounds react by exchanging their ions (or groups of atoms) to form two new compounds.

1. Precipitation Reactions:

Forms an insoluble solid (precipitate).

Activity 1.10: Sodium Sulfate + Barium Chloride

Procedure: Take ~3mL sodium sulfate solution. In another test tube, ~3mL barium chloride solution. Mix.

Observation: A white insoluble substance (precipitate) is formed. Na2SO4(aq) + BaCl2(aq) → BaSO4(s)↓ + 2NaCl(aq) (Sodium sulfate + Barium chloride → Barium sulfate (white ppt) + Sodium chloride)

The white precipitate BaSO4 is formed by reaction of SO42- and Ba2+ ions.

Tips & Tricks! 💡 Spotting Precipitation

Look for reactions between two aqueous solutions (aq) that form a solid (s). Solubility rules (you'll learn more later) help predict precipitates, but for now, look for the "(s)" symbol in product side!

Recall Activity 1.2 (Lead(II) nitrate + Potassium iodide): Pb(NO3)2(aq) + 2KI(aq) → PbI2(s)↓ + 2KNO3(aq) (Yellow precipitate of PbI2).

Oxidation: Gain of Oxygen / Loss of Hydrogen / Loss of Electrons.

Reduction: Loss of Oxygen / Gain of Hydrogen / Gain of Electrons.

My Short Explanation 💬 Redox Simply

Redox is all about electrons changing hands! If something loses electrons, it's oxidized (LEO the lion says GER: Loss of Electrons is Oxidation, Gain of Electrons is Reduction). Or remember OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).

The one giving away oxygen (or gaining hydrogen) is the oxidizing agent (it makes the other guy oxidize). The one taking oxygen (or giving hydrogen) is the reducing agent.

Activity 1.11: Heating Copper Powder

Procedure: Heat a china dish with ~1g copper powder.

Observation: Surface of copper powder becomes coated with black copper(II) oxide (CuO). 2Cu(s) + O2(g) --Δ→ 2CuO(s) (Copper is oxidized as it gains oxygen).

If hydrogen gas is passed over this heated CuO, the black coating turns brown (reverse reaction, Cu is formed): CuO(s) + H2(g) --Δ→ Cu(s) + H2O(l) Here, CuO loses oxygen (reduced), H2 gains oxygen (oxidized).

Other Examples:

  • ZnO(s) + C(s) → Zn(s) + CO(g) (ZnO reduced, C oxidized)
  • MnO2(s) + 4HCl(aq) → MnCl2(aq) + 2H2O(l) + Cl2(g) (MnO2 reduced, HCl oxidized to Cl2)

Think & Discuss (Based on NCERT Pg 13)

Recall Activity 1.1 (Mg ribbon burning). Is magnesium being oxidised or reduced? (Answer: Oxidised, as it gains oxygen).

✨ Everyday Chemistry: Oxidation's Impact

Corrosion is when a metal is attacked by environmental substances. It's an oxidation process.

My Short Explanation 💬 Corrosion

Think of corrosion like nature trying to "un-refine" metals, taking them back to their more stable ore forms (often oxides or sulfides). Rusting of iron is the most common example we see.

Common Examples:

  • Rusting of Iron: Reddish-brown Fe2O3·xH2O.
  • Tarnishing of Silver: Black Ag2S.
  • Green coating on Copper.

Tips & Tricks! 💡 Fighting Corrosion

The key is to create a barrier between the metal and air/moisture (painting, oiling) or use a more reactive "sacrificial" metal (galvanizing).

Prevention Methods: Painting, oiling, greasing, galvanizing, chrome plating, anodizing, alloying.

Rancidity: Oxidation of fats/oils in food, causing bad smell/taste.

My Short Explanation 💬 Rancidity

Oxygen attacks the fats and oils in your chips or nuts, making them taste stale or "off." That's rancidity. Removing oxygen (like in chip bags) or adding "bodyguards" (antioxidants) helps prevent it.

Prevention: Adding antioxidants (substances preventing oxidation like BHA, BHT), packaging in nitrogen gas, using airtight containers, refrigeration, storing away from light.

🔥❄️ Energy in Action: Exothermic & Endothermic Journeys

My Short Explanation 💬 Exo vs. Endo

Exothermic = Heat Exits (reaction feels hot).

Endothermic = Heat Enters (reaction feels cold or needs continuous heating).

Exothermic Reactions: Heat Out! 🔥

Reactions that release heat energy.

E.g., Burning fuel (CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g) + Heat), respiration (C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) → 6CO2(aq) + 6H2O(l) + Energy).

Endothermic Reactions: Heat In! ❄️

Reactions that absorb heat energy.

E.g., Photosynthesis, decomposition of CaCO3 (CaCO3(s) + Heat → CaO(s) + CO2(g)).

💡 Conceptual Understanding Questions

1. Why is respiration considered an exothermic reaction?

Respiration is considered an exothermic reaction because energy is released during this process. Glucose combines with oxygen in the cells of our body to produce carbon dioxide, water, and energy. This energy is utilized by the body to perform various life processes.

C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) → 6CO2(aq) + 6H2O(l) + Energy

2. What happens when quicklime is added to water? Write the chemical equation.

When quicklime (calcium oxide, CaO) is added to water, it reacts vigorously to form slaked lime (calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)2) and releases a large amount of heat. This is a combination reaction and an exothermic reaction.

CaO(s) + H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq) + Heat

3. Explain why a magnesium ribbon should be cleaned before burning in air.

Magnesium is a reactive metal. It slowly reacts with oxygen in the air to form a layer of magnesium oxide (MgO) on its surface. This layer is quite stable and can prevent or slow down the burning of magnesium. Therefore, the magnesium ribbon is cleaned with sandpaper to remove this protective layer of magnesium oxide so that the underlying metal can be exposed to air for combustion.

4. Can a displacement reaction be a redox reaction? Explain with an example.

Yes, most displacement reactions are also redox reactions. In a displacement reaction, a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. This involves changes in the oxidation states of the reacting species.

Example: Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s)

  • Iron (Fe) is oxidized from an oxidation state of 0 to +2 (in FeSO4). (Loss of electrons)
  • Copper (Cu) in CuSO4 (oxidation state +2) is reduced to Cu (oxidation state 0). (Gain of electrons)

Since both oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously, it is a redox reaction.

5. What is the difference between a "skeletal" chemical equation and a "balanced" chemical equation? Why is balancing necessary?

A skeletal chemical equation is an unbalanced chemical equation. It simply represents the reactants and products involved in a chemical reaction using their symbols and formulae, without considering the number of atoms of each element on both sides. Example: Mg + O2 → MgO.

A balanced chemical equation has an equal number of atoms of each element on both the reactant and product sides. It adheres to the Law of Conservation of Mass. Example: 2Mg + O2 → 2MgO.

Balancing is necessary to satisfy the Law of Conservation of Mass, which states that matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. The total mass of reactants must equal the total mass of products, which implies that the number of atoms of each element must remain the same throughout the reaction.

✏️ Short Answer Questions

1. What do you mean by a precipitation reaction? Give an example.

A precipitation reaction is a type of chemical reaction in which two soluble salts in aqueous solution combine, and one of the products is an insoluble salt called a precipitate, which settles down.

Example: When aqueous sodium chloride (NaCl) is added to aqueous silver nitrate (AgNO3), a white precipitate of silver chloride (AgCl) is formed.

AgNO3(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s)↓ + NaNO3(aq)

2. Why does the colour of copper sulphate solution change when an iron nail is dipped in it?

Iron is more reactive than copper. When an iron nail is dipped in a blue copper sulphate solution, iron displaces copper from the copper sulphate solution. This forms iron(II) sulphate, which is pale green in solution, and solid copper metal is deposited. The blue colour of copper sulphate fades and a greenish colour appears.

Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) (Blue) → FeSO4(aq) (Pale Green) + Cu(s) (Reddish-brown)

3. In the refining of silver, the recovery of silver from silver nitrate solution involved displacement by copper metal. Write down the reaction involved.

Copper is more reactive than silver. Therefore, copper will displace silver from silver nitrate solution. The reaction is:

Cu(s) + 2AgNO3(aq) → Cu(NO3)2(aq) + 2Ag(s)

In this reaction, copper displaces silver, forming copper(II) nitrate solution and solid silver.

📄 Long Answer Questions

1. Explain the different types of decomposition reactions with one example for each. How are these reactions different from combination reactions?

Decomposition reactions are those in which a single compound breaks down to give two or more simpler substances. These reactions require energy in the form of heat, light, or electricity.

Types of decomposition reactions:

  • Thermal Decomposition: Decomposition by heat.
    Example: Decomposition of calcium carbonate (limestone) on heating.
    CaCO3(s) --Δ→ CaO(s) + CO2(g)
  • Electrolytic Decomposition (Electrolysis): Decomposition by passing electricity.
    Example: Electrolysis of water.
    2H2O(l) --Electricity→ 2H2(g) + O2(g)
  • Photolytic Decomposition (Photolysis): Decomposition by light.
    Example: Decomposition of silver chloride in sunlight.
    2AgCl(s) --Sunlight→ 2Ag(s) + Cl2(g)

Difference from Combination Reactions:

Decomposition reactions are the opposite of combination reactions. In combination reactions, two or more substances combine to form a single new substance (A + B → AB). In decomposition reactions, a single substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances (AB → A + B). Combination reactions are generally exothermic, while decomposition reactions are generally endothermic (require energy input).

2. (a) What is meant by oxidation and reduction? Explain with an example involving the gain/loss of oxygen and another example involving the gain/loss of hydrogen.
(b) Identify the substances oxidized, substances reduced, oxidizing agents, and reducing agents in the following reactions:
(i) MnO2(s) + 4HCl(aq) → MnCl2(aq) + 2H2O(l) + Cl2(g)
(ii) Fe2O3(s) + 3CO(g) → 2Fe(s) + 3CO2(g)

(a) Oxidation and Reduction:

Oxidation is a process that involves:

  • Gain of oxygen. Example: 2Cu(s) + O2(g) → 2CuO(s) (Copper gains oxygen to form copper oxide).
  • Loss of hydrogen. Example: H2S(g) + Cl2(g) → 2HCl(g) + S(s) (Hydrogen sulphide loses hydrogen to form sulphur).
  • Loss of electrons.

Reduction is a process that involves:

  • Loss of oxygen. Example: CuO(s) + H2(g) → Cu(s) + H2O(l) (Copper oxide loses oxygen to form copper).
  • Gain of hydrogen. Example: Cl2(g) + H2(g) → 2HCl(g) (Chlorine gains hydrogen to form hydrogen chloride).
  • Gain of electrons.

(b) Identifying Oxidized/Reduced Substances and Agents:

(i) MnO2(s) + 4HCl(aq) → MnCl2(aq) + 2H2O(l) + Cl2(g)

  • Substance Oxidized: HCl (Hydrogen chloride loses hydrogen / Chlorine's oxidation state increases from -1 in HCl to 0 in Cl2).
  • Substance Reduced: MnO2 (Manganese dioxide loses oxygen / Manganese's oxidation state decreases from +4 in MnO2 to +2 in MnCl2).
  • Oxidizing Agent: MnO2 (It oxidizes HCl by accepting electrons/causing loss of H).
  • Reducing Agent: HCl (It reduces MnO2 by donating electrons/causing loss of O).

(ii) Fe2O3(s) + 3CO(g) → 2Fe(s) + 3CO2(g)

  • Substance Oxidized: CO (Carbon monoxide gains oxygen to form CO2).
  • Substance Reduced: Fe2O3 (Iron(III) oxide loses oxygen to form Fe).
  • Oxidizing Agent: Fe2O3 (It oxidizes CO by giving oxygen).
  • Reducing Agent: CO (It reduces Fe2O3 by taking oxygen).

📝 Key Concepts Recap

Important Definitions & Concepts

Chemical Reaction: Process involving rearrangement of atoms to form new substances (products) from original substances (reactants). Governed by the Law of Conservation of Mass.

Indicators of a Chemical Reaction:

  • Change in state
  • Change in color
  • Evolution of a gas
  • Change in temperature (exothermic/endothermic)
  • Formation of a precipitate

Chemical Equation: Symbolic representation using chemical formulas. Must be balanced to ensure the number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides of the equation. Example: 2Mg(s) + O2(g) → 2MgO(s).

Types of Chemical Reactions:

  • Combination: Two or more reactants combine to form a single product.
    General form: A + B → AB
    Example: CaO(s) + H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq) (Quicklime + Water)
  • Decomposition: A single reactant breaks down into two or more simpler products. Usually requires energy.
    General form: AB → A + B
    • Thermal (by heat): CaCO3(s) --Δ→ CaO(s) + CO2(g) (Limestone decomposition)
    • Electrolytic (by electricity): 2H2O(l) --Electricity→ 2H2(g) + O2(g) (Electrolysis of water)
    • Photolytic (by light): 2AgCl(s) --Sunlight→ 2Ag(s) + Cl2(g) (Silver chloride decomposition)
  • Displacement: A more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound.
    General form: A + BC → AC + B
    Example: Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s) (Iron nail in copper sulfate)
  • Double Displacement: Exchange of ions between two reactants to form two new compounds.
    General form: AB + CD → AD + CB
    Example (Precipitation): Na2SO4(aq) + BaCl2(aq) → BaSO4(s)↓ + 2NaCl(aq)
  • Redox (Oxidation-Reduction): Reactions involving simultaneous oxidation and reduction.
    • Oxidation: Gain of oxygen, loss of hydrogen, or loss of electrons.
    • Reduction: Loss of oxygen, gain of hydrogen, or gain of electrons.
    • Substance oxidized is the reducing agent.
    • Substance reduced is the oxidizing agent.
    • Example: CuO(s) + H2(g) → Cu(s) + H2O(l) (CuO is reduced, H2 is oxidized).

Energy Changes in Reactions:

  • Exothermic Reactions: Release heat energy to the surroundings (e.g., burning of methane, respiration). The surroundings get warmer.
  • Endothermic Reactions: Absorb heat energy from the surroundings (e.g., decomposition of limestone, photosynthesis). The surroundings get cooler or continuous energy supply is needed.

Oxidation in Everyday Life:

  • Corrosion: Gradual deterioration of metals by the action of air, moisture, or chemicals. Example: Rusting of iron (formation of hydrated iron(III) oxide, Fe2O3·xH2O).
  • Rancidity: Oxidation of fats and oils in food items, leading to unpleasant smell and taste. Prevented by using antioxidants, flushing with nitrogen gas, refrigeration, or airtight containers.
